4.1 Measures of Central Tendency: Mean, Mode and Median

When statisticians describe and compare data sets, they often look for one number to represent each data set. This number is known as an average or a measure of centre. Measures of centre are sometimes called measures of location. There are different ways of working out the average. We will look at three averages: the mean, the mode and the median.

The Mean

The mean of the set {2, 2, 3, 5, 9, 9} is:

Mean = \(\frac{2+2+3+5+9+9}{6} = \frac{30}{6} = 5\)

The Median

Ranking the set {4, 7, 11, 9, 12, 10, 8, 11, 14, 2, 6} gives {2, 4, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 11, 12, 14}.

The number in the middle of the ranked set is called the median, which in this case equals 9.

What happens if the set contains an even number of values? For example, the set {1, 2, 3, 4} contains an even number of values.

Median = \(\frac{2 + 3}{2} = \frac{5}{2} = 2.5\)

Worked Example 8.10

The area of a rectangular lawn is \(50 m^2\). The length of the lawn is twice its width. Find the perimeter of the lawn.

Solution

The length of the lawn is twice its width.

If the width \((w) = x\), then the length \((l) = 2x\)

Area of lawn \(= lw = 50m^2.\)

Fill in the dimensions:

\((2x)(x) = 50\)

\(\quad\;\;\, 2x^2 = 50\)

\(\quad\;\;\;\;x^2 = 25\)

\(\quad\;\;\,\therefore x = 5\)

Width \((w) = x = 5\) Width \(= 5m\)

Length \((l) = 2x = 2(5) = 10\) Length \(= 10m\)

Perimeter \(= 2(l) + 2(w) = 2(5) + 2(10)\)

\(\therefore\) Perimeter \(= 30m\)

Worked Example 8.25

A cylinder of wax of radius 12 cm and height 21 cm is melted down into two candles, one in the shape of a sphere and the other in the shape of a cone with a radius of 7 cm and a height of 5 cm.

Assuming no wax is wasted in the melting, calculate the radius of the sphere to the nearest cm.

Solution

Volume of the cylinder \(= \pi r^2h\)
\(= \pi(12)^2(21)\)
∴ Volume of cylinder \(= 3,024 \pi cm^3\)

Volume of the cylinder = volume of the sphere + volume of cone

∴ Volume of sphere + volume of cone \(= 3,024 \pi cm^3\)

\(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 + \frac{1}{3} \pi r^2h\) \(= 3,024 \pi\)
\(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 + \frac{1}{3} \pi (7)^2(5)\) \(=3,024\)
\(\frac{4}{3} r^3 + 81 \frac{2}{3}\) \(=3,024\)
\(\frac{4}{3} r^3\) \(=3,024 - 81\frac{2}{3}\)
\(\frac{4}{3} r^3\) \(=2,942\frac{1}{3}\)
\(r^3\) \(=\frac{2,942\frac{1}{3}}{\frac{4}{3}}\)
\(r^3\) \(= 2,206 \frac{3}{4}\)
\(r\) \(=\sqrt[3]{2,206 \frac{3}{4}}\)
\(r\) \(= 13.0192 cm\)
\(\therefore r \) \(= 13 cm\) (nearest cm)

Worked Example 11.3

The equation of the circle is \(9x^2 + 9y^2 = 100\)

(i) Find the centre of \(s\).

(ii) Find the radius length of \(s\).

Solution

(i) Dividing both sides of the equation of \(s\) by 9 gives:

\(x^2 + y^2 = \frac{100}{9}\)

The equation is now in the familiar \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\) form.

The centre of \(s\) is (0, 0).

(ii) The radius of \(s\) is:

\(r^2 = \frac{100}{9} \Rightarrow r = \sqrt{\frac{100}{9}}\), as \(r \gt 0\)

\(\Rightarrow r = \frac{10}{3}\)
